Date:
29 March 2010
Subject
Science and Technology

2857 (2010). John Boscawen to the Minister of Research Science and Technology (29 Mar 2010): Further to his answer to written question number 01321 (2010), can he refer to any pre-1981 paper in the peer-reviewed international literature recommending techniques for constructing composites of multiple parts of an inhomogeneous record; or any post-1993 paper that expressly approves of the methodology proposed in the thesis of J Salinger (1981)?
Dr Wayne Mapp (Minister of Research Science and Technology) replied: Yes, I referred to a pre-1981 paper in my answer to written question 01321 (2010). That paper is Kohler, M.A., 1949. Double-mass analysis for testing the consistency of records and for making adjustment. Bulletin American Meteorological Society, 30, 188-189. NIWA have provided me with the following names of papers post 1993: A paper by Easterling, DE and Petersen, TC, 1995 A new method for detecting undocumented discontinuities in climatological time series, Int. Journal of Climatology, 15, 369-377, utilises the same methodology as Salinger and extends it. The Third Edition of the Guide to Climatological Processes, published by the World Meterological Organization’s Commission for Climatology in 2007 references and builds on the methodology used by Salinger. Easterling and Peterson (1995) explain the basic philosophy of site adjustments thus: “The determination of homogeneity must be made by comparison of time series from the station of interest (candidate) to those from nearby, closely related reference stations. Implicit in this methodology is the assumption that any regional climate change or fluctuations experienced by the candidate station will show up in the nearby reference stations as well.” Peterson et al (1998) makes a similar statement, and references four of Salinger’s papers, although not specifically the 1981 thesis. The IPCC Second Assessment (1995) Working Group I chapter on “Observed Climate Variability and Change” also cites several papers by Salinger where climate analyses are presented that draw on homogenised records. References in addition to those cited in answer to Question 01321: 1) Peterson et al (1998): “Homogeneity adjustments of in situ atmospheric climate data: A review”. International Journal of Climatology, 18: 1493-1517. 2) Nicholls, N., Gruza, G.V., Jouzel, J., Karl, T.R., Ogallo, L.A., and Parker, D.E., 1996: Observed Climate Variability and Change. In: Climate Change 1995: The Science of Climate Change. Contribution of Working Group I to the Second Assessment Report of the Intergovernmental Panel on Climate Change (Houghton, J.T., Meira Filho, L.G., Callander, B.A., Harris, N., Kattenberg, A., and Maskell, K. (eds.)), Cambridge University Press, Cambridge, United Kingdom and New York, NY, USA.
