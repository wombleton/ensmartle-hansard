Order Paper and questions

Questions for written answer

Content provider
Information
Date:
27 April 2010
Subject
Science and Technology

6181 (2010). Sue Kedgley to the Minister of Research Science and Technology (27 Apr 2010): Is it a requirement in NZ that scientists seek consent before undertaking research using human genetic material; if not, why not?
Dr Wayne Mapp (Minister of Research Science and Technology) replied: I am advised that depending upon the nature of the research, and the origin of the genetic material, various types of research are covered by different sets of New Zealand legislation and/or regulations. 1. Genetic material derived from human tissues collected from individuals Genetic material derived from human tissues collected from specific individuals is governed by the Human Tissue Act (2008). Informed consent is required under this Act for the collection of human tissue which is or includes human cells (and therefore genetic material). The Operational Standard for Ethics Committees mandates that consent from the individual must be given for research, unless the Committee is satisfied that the potential public benefit in allowing the research to proceed outweighs the very strong need to protect an individuals right to consent. Section 20 of the Human Tissue Act (2008) lists a number of circumstances under which informed consent is not required for the collection or use of human tissue. The Human Tissue Act does not cover sub-cellular material such as genetic material once it is outside the cell. 2. Genetic material derived from information contained in databases Modern technology allows human genetic material to be synthesised from information contained in databases (such as the US National Centre for Biotechnology Information Nucleotide database). This information is anonymous and readily available to the public and therefore does not require consent for its use. 3. Genetic material derived from established cell lines Gaining consent for the use of genetic material derived from established cell lines may not be practical in many circumstances. However, if an investigator wishes to establish a new cell line, then the Guidelines for the Use of Human Tissue for Future Unspecified Research Purposes are relevant. The guidelines apply to any information that is derived from human tissue. The guidelines provide the option of a one-off consent for the future use of tissue in research. These guidelines define human tissue as any material collected from a living or deceased person that is or includes human cells. However, for the purposes of these guidelines, human tissue does not include gametes and embryos and established cell lines derived from human embryos (these are covered by another set of guidelines). 4. Human genetic material administered to a human (clinical trials) For research involving administration of nucleic acids (the constituents of genetic material) to one or more human beings, the Medicines Act (1981) applies. If the “medicine” is to be used for the sole purpose of obtaining clinical and scientific information, then the investigator is required to seek permission for its use under Section 30 of the Medicines Act: “Exemption for a clinical trial”. Approval under Section 30 is given by the Director-General of Health on the recommendation of the Health Research Council of New Zealand (HRC). The recommendation to the Director-General of Health is made by the HRC’s Gene Technology Advisory Committee (GTAC). Appropriate consent must be obtained from candidates for the proposed treatment. 5. Research involving modification of human genetic material The Hazardous Substances and New Organisms Act (1996) (HSNO Act) regulates new organisms, which includes genetically modified organisms. Under the HSNO Act, all other relevant approvals (including ethics committee approval) must be obtained before the research can proceed. If human genetic material is used in genetic modification then an approval under the HSNO Act must be gained before the research can commence.
