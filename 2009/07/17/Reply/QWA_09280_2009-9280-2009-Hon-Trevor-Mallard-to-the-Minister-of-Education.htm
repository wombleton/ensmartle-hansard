Subject
Education

9280 (2009). Hon Trevor Mallard to the Minister of Education (17 Jul 2009): Further to her answer to QWA 6991 (2009), what are the changes in assumptions which led to each of the changes in the forecasts ?
Hon Anne Tolley (Minister of Education) replied: Forecasting changes in the 2009 March Baseline Update comprised the following (all figures $000, GST exclusive): School Operations 2008/09 2009/10 2010/11 2011/12 2012/13 Changes in Rolls (5,224) 2,374 1,109 979 1,579 Changes in Rates (104) 3,825 2,896 2,894 2,889 Correspondence School 2,714 2,714 2,714 2,714 2,714 TOTAL (2,614) 8,913 6,719 6,587 7,182 The increases in forecast school operations funding (Primary Education, Secondary Education and Special Needs Support) across the forecast period were mainly due to: • Changes in Roll Distribution – secondary rolls were projected to decrease for the outyears at a lower rate than previously forecast due to a predicted rise in retention. Primary rolls were projected to increase at the same rate but to a lower extent than anticipated in the previous forecast due to the National School Roll Projections which predicted lower rolls due to falling migration. • Changes in Rates – maintenance was increased due to the previous year’s 3.1% inflation adjustment which had not been applied to maintenance in the previous forecast, as well as a number of back-payments owing to schools, and payments for swimming pools. • Correspondence School – increased costs of secondary education programmes for the correspondence school owing to higher than expected numbers of students. Teacher Salaries 2008/09 2009/10 2010/11 2011/12 2012/13 Full-time Teacher Equivalents (9,560) (27,502) (29,029) (18,243) (4,965) Rates 5,500 13,670 13,980 14,004 14,056 Holiday Pay: FTTEs (1,736) (1,352) 918 3,091 2,513 Operating Impact (5,796) (15,184) (14,131) (1,148) 11,604 Forecast changes for teacher salaries funding (Primary Education, Secondary Education and Special Needs Support) were due to: • Full-time Teacher Equivalents (FTTEs) – latest national school roll projections were incorporated. • Rates – the change was a result of better data based on actual fortnightly rates in the second tranche of the 2007 to 2010 Collective Agreements. • Holiday Pay: FTTEs – the changes to teacher numbers above also had a flow on effect for the cost of teacher holiday pay at the end of the financial year. School Transport 2008/09 2009/10 2010/11 2011/12 2012/13 Daily and Technology Services 3,265 4,933 5,062 5,313 5,728 Direct Resourced Services 1,747 4,944 4,884 4,740 4,680 Special Needs – Taxi 2,126 1,961 2,536 3,122 3,615 Other 37 (61) (65) (65) (61) Operating Impact 7,175 11,777 12,417 13,110 13,962 The increases in the transport forecast across the forecast period were owing to a combination of: • Daily and Technology Services – the change in 2008/09 was the result of a combination of increased contractual rates in the new tender round, higher than forecast global rating adjustments based on the September 2008 quarter, which had not yet reflected the impact of later falls in fuel prices, partly offset by reduction in the number of daily routes. Contract rates in subsequent years reflected CPI changes. • Special Needs Taxi – the 2008 contract provided for taxi operators to receive updated contractual rates over time when a certain threshold was met (e.g. student numbers or route distances increased by 2%). • Directly Resourced Services (DRS) – adjustments reflecting more daily routes than expected moving to DRS. • Other - forecasts for Special Needs Conveyance, Conveyance Allowance and kura kaupapa Māori being refined slightly since the 2008 October Baseline Update and correcting information . Early Childhood Education 2008/09 2009/10 2010/11 2011/12 2012/13 Volume of Hours 30,454 36,191 34,230 36,201 41,290 Hours Composition 14,685 17,974 18,433 (3,757) (8,836) 20 Hours ECE Take-Up Rates (615) (947) (1,032) (1,178) (1,314) Small Components (1,880) (117) (120) (129) (131) Operating Impact 42,644 53,101 51,511 31,137 31,009 A significant increase was expected in the early childhood education (ECE) forecast. The key factors in this forecast were: • Volumes of Hours – a combination of growth in participation and population growth. Adjustments were made to 2008/09 to take into account year-to-date figures for Over 2s which were above the 2008 October Baseline Update forecast. From 2009/10 onwards it was expected the Over 2s would grow in line with population growth. In Under 2s there was an adjustment to forecast for population growth. • Hours Composition – a reduction in the number of sessional kindergartens, from the current total of 424 to approximately 260 by 2012/13. All-day services were also predicted to move up the funding bands faster than expected in the 2008 October Baseline Update forecast. • 20 Hours ECE Take-up Rates – an increase in the proportion of hours attributable to the 20 Hours ECE scheme for children aged two and over attending quality Te Kōhanga Reo services from 20 to 25 per cent. The 100% all-day proportion was lowered from 74 to 72 per cent. • Small Components – the year to date under-expenditure in equity funding of $1.6 million was expected to continue in 2008/09 with fewer services receiving equity funding. The rolls at the Correspondence School were expected to keep falling, reducing the forecast by approximately $0.2 million each year. Other minor changes were also forecast.
