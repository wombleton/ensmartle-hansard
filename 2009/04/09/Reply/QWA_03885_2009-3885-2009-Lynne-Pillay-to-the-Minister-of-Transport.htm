Date:
9 April 2009
Subject
Transport

3885 (2009). Lynne Pillay to the Minister of Transport (09 Apr 2009): Is any work being undertaken to facilitate affordable accessible public transport for people with disabilities?
Hon Steven Joyce (Minister of Transport) replied: The NZ Transport Agency Board approved Requirements for urban buses in New Zealand in December 2008. These requirements aim to enhance the attractiveness of urban public transport vehicles to encourage increased usage, with a particular emphasis on improving accessibility. The Ministry of Transport and the NZ Transport Agency continue to oversee the implementation by regional councils of the Total Mobility Scheme. This Scheme provides subsidised taxi services for people who are unable to use public transport due to the nature of their disability. The Ministry of Transport has been developing an implementation plan of the recommendations in the Human Rights Commission report, The Accessible Journey. However, this work has slowed significantly due to other priorities. The Ministry is preparing a briefing that considers these recommendations in light of the government objectives of improving economic efficiency and productivity. The NZ Transport Agency Board has recently approved an accessibility project to take place in Gisborne. This project will trial a methodology to improve integration of land use planning and transport. This will potentially lead to improved economic and educational outcomes for communities. A major stakeholder in this work is CCS Disability in the Gisborne district. Regional Transport Committees have been established under the Land Transport Management Act 2008. This legislation requires that Regional Transport Committees represent the five objectives of transport. Recently a training day took place to ensure that representatives are aware of their role and are able to influence the planning process. One person on this committee represents access and mobility. Their aim is to ensure that the transport system increasingly provides affordable and reliable community access. The NZ Transport Agency develops a range of guidelines for regional councils and territorial authorities. An example of accessibility guidelines includes RTS 14 Guidelines for facilities for blind and vision-impaired pedestrians. These are developed in consultation with the NZ Association of Blind Citizens, the Royal NZ Foundation of the Blind and the Disabled Persons Assembly. These guidelines are not mandatory, but encourage local government to implement accessible infrastructure.
